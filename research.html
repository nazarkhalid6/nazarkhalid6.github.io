Floods, community infrastructure, and children’s learning losses in rural India

Economics of Education Review, 2025.

Using panel data from rural India, this paper shows that flood exposure lowers children’s test scores, but that robust local infrastructure substantially mitigates these learning losses.

We link household and child-level survey data to fine-grained flood exposure measures to study how environmental shocks shape learning in rural India. Exploiting variation in flood incidence across villages and time, we estimate the association between flood exposure and children’s math and reading scores. We then examine how these learning losses vary with village-level infrastructure, including roads, markets, health centres, and schools. The results show sizeable average losses in test scores following floods, with markedly smaller declines in villages that have stronger physical and social infrastructure, highlighting the importance of local capacity for buffering educational impacts of climate shocks.

Floods and children’s education in rural India

RSF: The Russell Sage Foundation Journal of the Social Sciences, 2024.

This article examines how floods shape children’s grade progression and learning, and how these effects differ by caste, religion, gender, and household resources.

Drawing on longitudinal data from rural India, we investigate whether flood exposure disrupts school participation and learning trajectories, and whether these disruptions are distributed unevenly across social groups. We estimate how grade-for-age, basic literacy, and numeracy outcomes change following flood events, and test for heterogeneity by caste, religion, gender, and socioeconomic status. The analysis shows that floods depress school progress for all children but that socially marginalized groups are disproportionately exposed. Conditional on exposure, learning losses are not uniformly larger for marginalized children, suggesting that differential exposure is a key driver of stratified educational outcomes.

Gender, government intervention, and slow LPG adoption in rural north India

World Development, 2021.

We study why subsidised LPG connections do not readily translate into sustained clean fuel use, highlighting the role of refill affordability, supply reliability, and intra-household power.

Using survey and qualitative data from rural north India, this paper examines the implementation and consequences of a major LPG subsidy programme. We document high uptake of subsidised LPG connections but continued reliance on solid fuels for cooking. Analyses show that refill costs, supply-side bottlenecks, and gendered control over resources constrain sustained use of LPG. The findings suggest that clean energy transitions require attention not only to connection access but also to recurring costs and gendered decision-making within households.

Measurement of population mental health: Survey methods and outcomes from India

Health Policy and Planning, 2021.

Evaluates the feasibility and biases of mental health measurement through phone surveys, with guidance on instrument design in low-resource settings.

We assess how different modes and instruments perform in measuring population mental health in India using large-scale phone surveys. The study compares symptom scales, explores non-response and selection, and examines correlates of distress. Findings highlight both the promise and pitfalls of remote mental health measurement and provide practical recommendations for survey design in low- and middle-income contexts.

When women eat last: Discrimination at home and women's mental health

PLOS ONE, 2021.

Links discriminatory food allocation within households to symptoms of psychological distress among women in rural north India.

Using survey data from married women, this paper documents norms around intra-household food distribution, focusing on the practice of women eating last. We relate these norms to validated measures of mental health and find that women who consistently eat last report worse psychological outcomes, even after accounting for socioeconomic and demographic factors. The results underscore how everyday gendered practices can affect mental wellbeing.

Persisting Prejudice: Measuring Attitudes and Outcomes by Caste and Gender

CASTE: A Global Journal on Social Exclusion, 2020.

Combines attitudinal and behavioural measures to document the persistence of caste and gender prejudice in contemporary India.

We use survey experiments and attitudinal questions to measure explicit and implicit prejudice by caste and gender. The study links these measures to reported behaviours and outcomes, showing that discriminatory attitudes remain widespread and are associated with tangible disadvantages for marginalised groups. The findings point to the limits of legal reform in eroding social hierarchy.

Revisiting Open Defecation: Evidence from a Panel Survey in Rural North India, 2014–18

Economic and Political Weekly, 2020.

Uses panel data to assess progress in reducing open defecation during the Swachh Bharat Mission and documents persistent gaps.

This article follows households over four years to evaluate changes in toilet ownership and open defecation in the context of a major rural sanitation campaign. Despite increases in toilet coverage, many individuals continue to defecate in the open. We explore reasons for non-use and show how social and cultural factors, alongside programme implementation, shape sanitation behaviour.

Persistence of Solid Fuel Use in Rural North India

Economic and Political Weekly, 2020.

Analyses why households continue to rely on solid fuels despite access to cleaner alternatives, with implications for energy policy.

We study patterns of cooking fuel use in rural north India, focusing on households that have access to LPG but continue to use wood or dung. The analysis highlights how fuel stacking, affordability constraints, and supply interruptions contribute to the persistence of solid fuel use, underscoring the need for policies that address both access and sustained use.

Experiences and Perceptions of Discrimination among Dalits and Muslims

Economic and Political Weekly, 2018.

Documents everyday experiences of discrimination and contrasts them with how often such experiences are reported and acknowledged.

Combining survey questions on everyday treatment with items on perceived discrimination, this paper examines how Dalit and Muslim respondents in north India describe and label their experiences. We show that discriminatory acts are common, but are not always named as “discrimination,” revealing gaps between lived experience and reported perceptions that matter for policy and politics.

Aadhaar, Biometric Authentication and the PDS in Jharkhand

Economic and Political Weekly, 2017.

Examines how biometric authentication affects access to subsidised food and highlights risks of exclusion under Aadhaar-linked welfare delivery.

We study the rollout of Aadhaar-based biometric authentication in the public distribution system (PDS) in Jharkhand. Drawing on household survey data and field interviews, the paper documents both reductions in some forms of leakage and new barriers to access, including authentication failures and increased transaction costs for poor households. The findings raise concerns about exclusion in technology-mediated welfare systems.
